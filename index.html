Executive Summary

The thesis: better in three dimensions

Non-Chinese OEMs are launching versions in China that are objectively better than their global counterparts across three customer-visible dimensions: more usable space, more advanced digital cockpits, and stronger battery stacks. Even so, in most cases, the “better car for China” still shares the same global electrical/electronic architecture.

This pattern is not anecdotal. Foreign manufacturers are reacting to a market in which their share has eroded rapidly. Foreign brands fell from 62% of passenger-car sales in China in 2020 to 31% in the first seven months of 2025, according to data cited by Reuters. One important factor in this regard is the renewal cycle of the models on sale (“the new cycle of refresh”). According to AlixPartners, Chinese electrified models sold domestically had an average age of 1.6 years, compared with 5.4 years for foreign brands. In other words, Chinese brands iterate faster, which allows them to offer models with stronger performance and more up-to-date features.

This is especially important when competing in electric vehicles, where batteries improve month by month; in software, where new integrations and functionalities are constantly emerging; and in the in-car experience, where new interior designs enable new configurations and use cases.

“Between 2024 and 2026, foreign brands have been forced into a sprint to avoid being pushed out of the market by the fierce competition from Chinese OEMs.”

Of course, this phenomenon is not symmetrical across every brand or every model. There are different stages. The thesis is very strong in the case of Audi and Mercedes-Benz, fairly strong in Hyundai, more nuanced in BMW, and only partial in Toyota.

As a result, China has become the most advanced technology and product showcase for several non-Chinese manufacturers. Time will tell whether these improvements are transferred to Western markets. Initial signals are pointing that this is happening, and to what extent. The fact that Chinese players are reaching record levels in exports already offers a first answer: the presence of Chinese models in foreign markets will force OEMs to follow them. The question is whether they will do so proactively or reactively.

Comparison Method

How we tested the thesis

To test the thesis, we compared pairs of models with a reasonable relationship in terms of segment and platform, although they do not always match exactly in body style or nameplate.

We focused on physical and functional elements: wheelbase, battery size, charging power, screen architecture, ADAS stack, and similar factors, since brands mix homologation cycles such as CLTC, WLTP and EPA, or change names depending on trim levels.

For that reason, when a Chinese-European model outperforms another model “only” in declared range, we do not consider it sufficient evidence. When it also performs better in battery capacity, space, onboard computing or assisted-driving functions, we consider it robust evidence.

We also distinguished between two types of “superiority”. The first is universal: more space, a larger battery, better charging capability, or a better interface. The second is localized: Chinese app stores, voice assistants adapted to the local ecosystem, HiCar integration, urban navigation assistance designed for Chinese traffic, or “memory NOA” features. This second category is still a real advantage, but it does not always translate automatically into an advantage outside China and, in particular, it makes direct comparison more difficult.

To structure the model analysis, we classified each model using three categories:

Direct comparables
The China model has a clear global counterpart: same model family, same platform, same product lineage or a recognisable long-wheelbase derivative.
Same base model China → Europe
The model is born in China and then adapted or exported to Europe.
China-only / China-first
The model is developed specifically for China, or launched first in China, without a clean global equivalent.

Deep-dive into specific models

Five models, five strategies

Each case illustrates a different stage of the thesis, from very strong direct comparables to more nuanced China-first products.

China-First
IONIQ V
Hyundai

The Hyundai IONIQ V should be understood as a China-first IONIQ product, not as a direct Chinese counterpart to the IONIQ 5 or IONIQ 6. The IONIQ 6 can be used as a global-family reference, but not as a strict model-to-model comparison.

The importance of the IONIQ V is that Hyundai is using China to launch a more digital and locally adapted interpretation of the IONIQ brand. The vehicle is positioned around a 27-inch 4K panoramic display, Snapdragon 8295P chipset, Dolby Atmos, an AI assistant and a China-specific ADAS stack developed with a local partner. It also announces more than 600 km of CLTC range.

Assessment
The IONIQ V is evidence that Hyundai is building a dedicated, more China-specific smart-EV product for that market, not evidence that Hyundai sells “the same car but better” in China.
Direct Comparable
iX3 Long Wheelbase
BMW

The BMW iX3 Long Wheelbase is a direct comparable to the global iX3 Neue Klasse. It belongs to the same product family and uses the same strategic EV architecture, but is adapted specifically for the Chinese market.

The Chinese version extends the wheelbase and puts more emphasis on rear-seat comfort, local digital services and a China-specific software experience. It also integrates more deeply with local ecosystems and traffic conditions, including China-specific navigation, voice assistant and driver-assistance development.

Assessment
The Chinese version should not be described as mechanically superior in every respect. Its advantage is more precise: it is the more locally optimised derivative of the same global platform, especially for rear-seat comfort and digital localisation.
Direct Comparable
Q6L e-tron
Audi

The Audi Q6L e-tron is one of the cleanest and strongest examples of a direct comparable. It is a Chinese long-wheelbase derivative of the global Audi Q6 e-tron.

Compared with the global Q6 e-tron, the Q6L e-tron adds a longer wheelbase, more rear-seat space, a larger 107 kWh battery and more than 700 km of CLTC range. It keeps the same broad premium EV logic (PPE platform, 800-volt architecture and high-power charging), but adapts the product more clearly to Chinese customer expectations.

Assessment: Very Strong
This case strongly supports the thesis. The Chinese-market model is not simply a local trim or software package. It is a physically differentiated version with more space, more battery capacity and a deeper China-specific digital and assistance layer.
Direct Comparable
CLA L
Mercedes-Benz

The Mercedes-Benz CLA L is a direct comparable to the global electric CLA. It is the Chinese long-wheelbase version of the same new-generation product family.

The Chinese CLA L receives a longer wheelbase and more emphasis on rear-seat comfort, while retaining the same major technical shift as the global model: the new MMA platform, MB.OS, 800-volt architecture and highly efficient electric powertrain. The difference is not that China receives a completely different vehicle architecture. The difference is that China receives the same new technical base with additional packaging and digital localisation for local expectations.

Assessment: Strong
A strong case for the thesis, but more incremental than Audi. It shows how Mercedes-Benz adapts a global model for China by adding rear-seat space and a China-fit digital experience, rather than creating a radically different EV.
China-Only / China-First
bZ3X
Toyota (GAC Toyota)

The Toyota bZ3X should be treated as a China-only / China-first case, not as a direct counterpart to the global Toyota bZ, bZ4X or C-HR+. It is a China-specific GAC Toyota smart EV, developed for local competitive conditions.

In China, Toyota is using local development, local ADAS suppliers, LiDAR in some versions, a 14.6-inch screen, four-zone voice interaction and a more explicit smart-EV positioning. The objective is not only to sell an electric vehicle, but to compete with Chinese brands on price, cockpit technology, assisted driving and digital experience.

Outside China, Toyota’s global bZ communication is more conventional: range, battery size, charging, safety and connectivity. That does not make the global product weak, but it shows that Toyota’s China product is being shaped by a different competitive standard.

Assessment: Partial
The bZ3X does not prove that Toyota sells “the same car but better” in China. It supports a more nuanced thesis: in China, Toyota is willing to deploy local technology partners and smart-EV features that are much less visible in its Western EV portfolio.

Recurring Patterns

Four patterns that repeat

When all these cases are considered together, four consistent patterns emerge.

Pattern 01
The long-wheelbase strategy
BMW, Audi and Mercedes-Benz continue to stretch cars for China because the perceived value of rear-seat space is higher there than in many Western markets.
Pattern 02
The local digital layer
Assistants, navigation, app stores, integration with Chinese ecosystems, and ADAS or assisted-driving systems calibrated for complex urban traffic.
Pattern 03
Co-creation with Chinese partners
In most cases, this means agreements with local hardware and software suppliers, especially in cockpit technology, assisted driving, mapping, connectivity, and artificial intelligence.
Pattern 04
The acceleration of the development cycle
Domestic competitive pressure in China is forcing foreign manufacturers to launch cars faster and with more software from day one.

Chinese consumers often receive better-adapted and, in several cases, objectively better products from foreign OEMs because the Chinese customer and OEMs elevate the standards and force OEMs to compete at a higher standard.

So What

Does better in China mean better everywhere?

Not better in every possible dimension. Not always better on core platforms. Not always better in absolute engineering quality. But better in the dimensions that matter most to the Chinese market.

Rear-seat space, digital cockpit, local software, assisted-driving stack, battery/range communication, price-value equation and speed of iteration. The core question is whether these attributes do or do not define a car as better in any market.

It is easy to argue that in many countries customers will select a car that has better specifications in these attributes. The evidence from China already points in that direction: Chinese players are reaching record levels in exports, and the presence of Chinese models in foreign markets will force OEMs to follow them.

The question, therefore, is whether they will do so proactively or reactively.
